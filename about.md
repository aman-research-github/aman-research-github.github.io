# About Me

Hello and welcome.

My name is Aman Awasthi, and I am a PhD candidate in Physics  Department at the Indian Institute of Technology Bombay. My research lies broadly in astrophysics and cosmology, with a focus on understanding the internal structure of stars and their observational signatures, as well as their connections to gravitational wave astrophysics and cosmology.

I work at the interface of theory, computation, and observation. My research involves modeling astrophysical systems, analyzing observational data, and developing computational tools to extract physical insights from complex phenomena.

---

## Research Overview

My current work spans several interconnected areas in modern astrophysics:

- **Stellar Physics and Helioseismology**  
  I study stellar interiors through oscillation modes, with particular interest in solar g-modes and their detectability. This work connects theoretical models of stellar structure with observational capabilities.

- **Gravitational Wave Astrophysics**  
  I investigate the prospects of detecting astrophysical signals using space-based detectors such as LISA, and explore how such observations can inform our understanding of stellar and compact object physics.

- **Observational Cosmology**  
  I am involved in ongoing work on the Hubble constant tension, analyzing supernova datasets to reassess systematic uncertainties in cosmological measurements.

- **Gravitational Microlensing**  
  I am developing data analysis pipelines for future missions such as the Roman Space Telescope to study microlensing events and probe compact objects and stellar populations.

In addition, I am interested in population synthesis studies and the application of numerical tools to model astrophysical systems.

## Scientific Work and Approach

My research is driven by a curiosity to understand how physical processes manifest across different scales—from stellar interiors to cosmological observations. I am particularly interested in problems where precise theoretical modeling can be directly tested with data.

My work typically involves:
- Computational modeling of astrophysical systems  
- Analysis of observational datasets  
- Use of numerical tools such as MESA, GYRE, and related frameworks
